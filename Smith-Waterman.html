The dynamic programming approach Smith-Waterman (1981) by Temple F. Smith and Michael S. Waterman computes unlike Needleman-Wunsch not the optimal, global alignment of two sequences, but the optimal, local alignment. This means that no more the whole sequences are aligned, but parts of both sequences such that the score is going to be optimal. Like in Needleman-Wunsch (1970) and other alignment algorithms, there exists two variants. One with minimizing scoring-function and also an equivalent maximizing variant.

The motivation is the same like in Needleman-Wunsch, you want do biological and medical examination. And therefore you need tools to compare two sequences with each other. Many present approaches are based on the Smith-Waterman algorithm.

In the first step of Smith-Waterman you initialize the left and the upper borders of a matrix with zeros. Then, like in Needleman-Wunsch you fill the matrix with the help of a recursion function $D_{i,j}$. The only difference to the Needleman-Wunsch recursion function is that you have a fourth case with a zero in your function. And the last step is the traceback-step in which you compute the alignments of the two entered sequences $S_1$ and $S_2$. Runtime and space are again like in the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm, so in $\mathcal O(nm)$, where $n=|S_1|$ and $m=|S_2|$.
